Leader–Follower Coordination on the Pololu 3pi+

📄 View full report PDF · 0.3 MB

Two small differential-drive robots that hold a formation without any wireless link — the follower tracks the leader purely from its infrared signal, the leader runs encoder odometry, and an IMU-driven collision response lets the pair swap roles on contact. Embedded C++ on the Pololu 3pi+ 32U4.

01 / Goal

Coordination without communication

The challenge was to make two robots behave as a coordinated pair using only their on-board sensing — no radio, no shared map, no message passing. The link between them is physical: the leader emits an infrared signal, the follower senses it and regulates its own motion to stay behind. On top of that base behaviour, the system detects collisions from its inertial sensor and dynamically swaps which robot leads, so the pair can recover and continue rather than deadlock.

  • 1Leader. Drive a controlled path, broadcast an IR beacon, and track its own pose by odometry.
  • 2Follower. Regulate distance and heading to the leader from IR alone, with two coupled proportional loops.
  • 3Fault response. Detect collisions on the IMU and hand off the leader role after a settling delay.

Honest framing: the figures below are the implementation's control constants and hardware parameters, not measured performance metrics — the deliverable was the working behaviour and its firmware, and no benchmark numbers are claimed.

02 / Leader

Driving, beaconing, and odometry

The leader drives a controlled path while continuously emitting its IR signal for the follower to lock onto, and estimates its own pose from wheel encoders so its motion is repeatable rather than open-loop. Straight-line driving is held with a proportional correction on the encoder difference, and the IR emitter runs on its own duty so the beacon and the drive loop don't interfere.

Collision detection

A contact event is declared when the IMU acceleration crosses a threshold (Z > 500, Y > 900 in raw units), gated by a 500 ms grace window so a single bump can't retrigger repeatedly. This is what feeds the role-switching logic — the leader knows it has been hit before it decides to hand off.

03 / Follower

Tracking the leader from IR alone

The follower runs two proportional loops at once on the infrared reading — one regulating distance (how strong the signal is) and one regulating heading (the left–right balance of the signal across its sensors). Combining them lets the robot both keep its spacing and turn to stay pointed at the leader, from a single sensing modality.

Dual-proportional control

  • Distance gain: KP_DIST = 0.1 — closes the gap toward the target IR strength
  • Turn gain: KP_TURN = 0.7 — steers toward the side with the stronger reading
  • Speed clamp: commanded wheel speed bounded to [−30, +50]
  • Signal-loss guard: below a minimum IR reading the follower stops rather than chasing noise

The turn loop is geared much harder than the distance loop (0.7 vs 0.1) — keeping the leader in view matters more than perfect spacing, because once the bearing is lost the distance signal becomes meaningless.

04 / Role switching

Recovering by swapping leader and follower

When the IMU flags a collision, the pair doesn't stall — after a 3 s settling delay the robots exchange roles: the former follower takes over beaconing and driving, the former leader falls in behind. The delay is deliberate, letting the physical disturbance settle so the handoff happens from a known state rather than mid-impact. It turns a failure mode (contact) into a defined, recoverable transition.

05 / Platform

Hardware & firmware structure

Everything runs on the Pololu 3pi+ 32U4, a differential-drive platform, with the firmware split into focused modules — encoders, kinematics/odometry, motor PWM, IR, and IMU — so each concern is isolated and independently testable rather than tangled into one loop.

06 / Takeaways

What I took from it

  • Coordination doesn't require communication — a single shared IR channel plus two proportional loops is enough to hold a formation, which is a useful reminder of how much behaviour emerges from sensing alone.
  • The gain split (turn ≫ distance) encodes a priority: keep the target in view first, regulate spacing second — the ordering of those loops matters more than either gain in isolation.
  • Designing the collision response as a defined role-switch, gated by a grace window and a settling delay, is what turns an unplanned contact into a recoverable state rather than a crash.
