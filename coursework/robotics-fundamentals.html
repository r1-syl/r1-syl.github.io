01 / Scope

What the unit covered

This is the groundwork unit that everything else in the MSc builds on: rigid-body transforms and the forward-kinematics chain, the Jacobian and its role in velocity mapping and singularities, inverse kinematics, an introduction to manipulator dynamics, and the trajectory generation that turns a desired path into a time-parameterised motion. The unit's assessment was an exam plus a written coursework; this page focuses on the implementation work I did to make those ideas concrete, not on the write-up itself.

Honest framing: the figures below are configuration parameters of deterministic trajectory/IK routines, not measured performance results — there is no benchmark to report here, and none is implied.

02 / Trajectory generation

A small toolkit of motion profiles

The core of the practical work was implementing the standard families of joint-space trajectory generators from scratch in Python and plotting their position, velocity, and acceleration profiles to see the trade-offs first-hand:

  • 1Trapezoidal / LSPB. Linear-segment-with-parabolic-blends — constant-velocity cruise bracketed by constant-acceleration ramps, the workhorse of point-to-point motion.
  • 2Cubic polynomial. Third-order rest-to-rest motion with matched position and velocity boundary conditions.
  • 3Quintic polynomial. Fifth-order motion that additionally pins the acceleration endpoints to zero, for a smoother, jerk-limited start and stop.
  • 4Via-point blending. Stitching multiple waypoints into one continuous trajectory with parabolic blends through each via-point.

Worked via-point example

  • Waypoints: [0, 40, 20, 30]° across three segments
  • Segment durations: [5, 10, 5] s, blended over 2.0 s at each via-point
  • Acceleration limit: a_max = 6 °/s²
  • Integration step: dt = 0.001 s

The blend duration is what trades positional fidelity at the waypoint against velocity continuity — a longer blend rounds the corner more but pulls the path further inside the via-point.

03 / Inverse kinematics

Solving a planar 2-DOF arm

To close the loop from Cartesian target back to joint angles, I implemented an analytical inverse kinematics solver for a 2-link planar manipulator in MATLAB (l₁ = l₂ = 0.1 m). The elbow angle follows from the law of cosines, with the cosine argument clamped to [−1, 1] so points just outside the reachable workspace (radius 0.2 m) degrade gracefully instead of producing a complex result.

A 2-link arm admits two solutions — elbow-up and elbow-down — for almost every reachable point. Surfacing both, and being explicit about which branch the solver returns, is the part of the exercise that connects the algebra back to a physical configuration the arm can actually strike.

04 / Takeaways

What I took from it

  • Trajectory generation is where path planning meets the actuator: a cubic and a quintic reach the same endpoints, but only the quintic keeps acceleration continuous — which is what the motor and the structure actually feel.
  • The blend duration on a via-point trajectory is a deliberate fidelity-vs-smoothness dial, not a detail — seeing the position/velocity/acceleration curves side by side is what makes that trade-off legible.
  • Clamping and branch selection in the IK solver are small but essential: they are what keep the foundational maths from breaking the moment a target sits at the edge of the workspace.
