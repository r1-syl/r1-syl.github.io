Sliding-Mode + LQR Hybrid Control of a 2-Link Manipulator

📄 View full report PDF · 2.0 MB

Designing, implementing, and validating a hybrid controller that pairs the disturbance rejection of sliding-mode control with the smoothness of an LQR — then stress-testing it against three baselines on a planar 2-link arm under bounded matched disturbances.

01 / Problem

The control problem

The task was to take a fully nonlinear 2-link planar manipulator, derive its dynamics from first principles, and design a controller that holds an accurate joint configuration while matched, bounded disturbance torques act on both joints (‖τ_d‖∞ ≤ [2.5, 1.5] N·m). The headline design target was a controller that is both robust (rejects disturbances like SMC) and smooth (low actuator effort like LQR) — the two goals that single-method controllers usually trade off.

I structured the work as five tasks, building from the physics up to an underactuated edge case.

  • 1Dynamic modelling. Full Lagrangian derivation of the mass, Coriolis, and gravity terms for the 2-link arm.
  • 2Control design. LQR about a linearised operating point, a boundary-layer SMC, and the hybrid blend of the two.
  • 3Simulink implementation. A 3-level manual-switch hierarchy so each controller runs on the identical plant.
  • 4Performance analysis. Quantitative comparison of SMC+LQR against SMC, FLC, and pure LQR.
  • 5Underactuated control. Collocated partial feedback linearisation when one joint is unactuated.
02 / Modelling

Dynamics & design

I derived the manipulator dynamics in the standard manipulator-equation form M(q)q̈ + C(q,q̇)q̇ + G(q) = τ using the Lagrangian, then linearised about the operating point q* = [1.25, 1.25] rad to obtain the state-space model used for LQR synthesis. The hybrid law keeps the LQR as the nominal stabiliser and adds a sliding-mode term that only does work when the trajectory leaves a thin boundary layer, so the chattering that plagues pure SMC is suppressed.

Design parameters

  • LQR weights: Qp = 100, Qd = 20, R = 1
  • PD-equivalent gains: Kp = 10, Kd = 2√10 ≈ 6.3246 (critical damping)
  • SMC switching gain: K = [3.5; 2.5] N·m, sized above the disturbance bound
  • Boundary layer: φ = 0.1 rad, sliding-surface slope λ = [5; 5]

The switching gain is deliberately set just above ‖τ_d‖∞ so the sliding term has just enough authority to dominate the worst-case disturbance without over-driving the actuators.

03 / Implementation

Simulink test rig

All four controllers share one plant model. I built a 3-level manual-switch hierarchy so that SMC+LQR, standalone SMC, fuzzy-logic control (FLC), and pure LQR can each be routed to the exact same manipulator under the exact same disturbance profile. This keeps the comparison fair — any difference in the results comes from the control law, not from a different test setup.

The disturbance was injected as a matched torque on both joints, bounded by the design limits, so the experiment directly probes the robustness the controllers were designed for.

The hybrid achieved the lowest steady-state error of all four controllers while also producing the smoothest torque signal — roughly 41% less total variation than standalone SMC, which is the chattering metric that matters most for real actuators. In other words, it kept SMC's robustness but shed SMC's biggest practical drawback.

05 / Extension

Underactuated case

The final task removed actuation from one joint. I applied collocated partial feedback linearisation (PFL) — linearising the dynamics of the actuated joint and letting the passive joint follow through the coupling terms. This is the harder, more realistic regime (fewer actuators than degrees of freedom) and it framed why the fully-actuated hybrid result is a baseline rather than an endpoint.

06 / Takeaways

What I took from it

  • A hybrid is only worth it if it keeps the strength of each parent while dropping a weakness — here, SMC's robustness minus its chattering, quantified by torque total-variation rather than asserted.
  • Fair comparison is an engineering artefact in itself: the shared-plant switch rig is what makes the four-way result trustworthy.
  • Reporting both tracking error and actuation smoothness matters — a controller that wins on RMSE but thrashes the actuator is not actually better.
