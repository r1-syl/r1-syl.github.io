Learning from Demonstration with DMP & GMR

📄 View full report PDF · 2.9 MB

Teaching a planar robot to reproduce a demonstrated motion — first encoding the trajectory with Dynamic Movement Primitives, then with Gaussian Mixture Regression, and finally driving it through inverse kinematics and a model-based controller in Simscape.

01 / Goal

Teaching a robot by example

The brief was to develop a full learning-from-demonstration pipeline for a planar robot: take a set of human-style demonstrations of a 2D motion, learn a compact generative model of it, and then make a simulated 2-DOF arm physically reproduce the motion. The interesting part is that the same demonstrated trajectory is encoded two different ways and the encodings are compared on equal terms, before the learned path is handed down through kinematics and dynamics to a Simscape robot.

  • 1Theory. The canonical / transformation system of a DMP and the GMM–GMR formulation.
  • 2Encoding & comparison. Implement a DMP, fit a GMM and regress with GMR, then compare reproduction accuracy.
  • 3Kinematic simulation. Analytical inverse kinematics that drive the arm along the learned Cartesian path.
  • 4Dynamic simulation. A model-based controller that tracks the joint trajectory on the full Simscape dynamics.
02 / Encoding

Two ways to encode a demonstration

A Dynamic Movement Primitive represents the motion as a stable second-order attractor (the transformation system) modulated by a learned forcing term driven by a phase variable (the canonical system). The forcing term is fit from the demonstrations, so the DMP can regenerate the motion while remaining robust to a changed start or goal.

I tuned the number of basis functions by sweeping K and tracking reproduction RMSE:

K = 9 sits at the knee of the curve — past it, extra basis functions stop buying accuracy and just add parameters. Configuration: nbData = 200, nbDemos = 5, nStates = 9, beta = 50.

The second encoding fits a Gaussian Mixture Model over the demonstrations via EM (seeded with rng(42) for reproducibility; the log-likelihood converges to ≈ −12,900 after ~90 iterations) and reconstructs the trajectory with Gaussian Mixture Regression — conditioning the joint distribution on the phase to retrieve the expected motion plus its covariance.

GMR reproduced the demonstration roughly 66.6% more accurately than the least-squares baseline, and — unlike a point-estimate fit — it also carries a covariance at every phase, which encodes how consistent the demonstrations were at each part of the motion.

04 / Kinematics

Driving the arm: inverse kinematics

To make the arm follow the learned Cartesian path, I solved the inverse kinematics analytically for the 2-link arm (L1 = L2 = 10 m). The elbow angle comes from the law of cosines, θ2 = atan2(√(1 − D²), D), where D is clamped to [−1, 1] so numerical noise can never push the argument out of the valid range.

Avoiding branch switching

A 2-link arm has an "elbow-up" and an "elbow-down" solution for most targets. Flipping between them mid-trajectory produces a discontinuous joint jump. By always selecting the positive (elbow-up) root, the joint solution stays on a single continuous branch for the whole path — no sudden reconfiguration. Tracking against the commanded motion held to the solver tolerance (≤ 1e-6).

05 / Dynamics

Tracking it on the real dynamics

The final stage closed the loop on the full Simscape dynamics with a model-based PD controller using inverse-dynamics feedforward:

τ = M(θ)θ̈_d + C(θ,θ̇)θ̇ + Kp(θ_d − θ) + Kd(θ̇_d − θ̇)

The feedback gains were placed for a critically-damped closed loop with natural frequency ωn = 10 rad/s and ζ = 1, giving Kp = 100·I, Kd = 20·I and a settling time of ≈ 0.4 s. Tracking the learned joint trajectory:

JointRMSE (rad)max |error| (rad)
Joint 11.36e-22.33e-2
Joint 23.49e-37.00e-3

Both joints tracked the demonstrated motion with small, bounded error — the residual reflects the finite bandwidth of the controller rather than a structural failure, since the inverse-dynamics feedforward already cancels most of the nonlinear coupling.

06 / Takeaways

What I took from it

  • A demonstration encoding should be judged on reproduction error, not on visual plausibility — the GMR-vs-LWLS and K-sweep numbers are what justified the design choices.
  • GMR's covariance output is more than a number: it tells you where the demonstrations agreed, which is exactly the information a downstream controller wants.
  • The pipeline only works end-to-end if each stage respects the next — clamping IK inputs and locking the elbow branch is what keeps the learned path from breaking when it reaches the dynamics.
